— UX Research & Design

Audible
Redesign

Audible advertises thousands of included titles — but finding them is surprisingly difficult. I ran a full UX research study to understand why, then designed and tested a solution that makes subscription value immediately visible to users.

My Role Solo UX Researcher & Designer
Methods Survey, Think-Aloud Interviews, Heuristic Evaluation, Prototyping
Tools Figma, PeerSurvey, Google Forms
Type Academic UX Research Project
20+ Survey participants
2 Think-aloud interviews
3 Prototypes tested
14 Survey questions

— 01

The Problem

For around $8 a month, Audible promises access to thousands of included audiobooks and Originals. But after using the app, I found it surprisingly difficult to find those titles — the interface primarily surfaces content that costs additional fees, with no clear way to filter for what's already included in your subscription.

This frustrated me enough that I eventually cancelled. But rather than just leaving, I wanted to understand whether this was a personal pain point or a systemic usability issue — and design a fix.

The core hypothesis: If users could easily see the thousands of titles they're already paying for, they would perceive the subscription as more valuable — and be less likely to cancel.

— 02

Needfinding

Before designing anything, I needed to validate whether other users experienced the same friction. I ran a multi-method research study over approximately one week, collecting both quantitative and qualitative data.

01

Online Survey — 20+ Participants

A 14-question survey built in PeerSurvey and Google Forms, shared via Instagram and a class discussion board. I recruited participants of varying ages, genders, locations, and levels of Audible experience to avoid a narrow sample. Questions mixed quantitative (nominal and ordinal scale) with qualitative open-response formats, designed to complete in 7–10 minutes.

02

Think-Aloud Interviews — 2 Participants

Two in-person interviews conducted in the Atlanta area with Audible novices of different ages. Participants navigated the app while narrating their actions, revealing where they naturally looked for included content — and where they got stuck. The think-aloud format surfaced confusion that post-task surveys alone would have missed.

03

Heuristic Evaluation

An individual evaluation of the existing Audible interface against established usability heuristics — identifying specific points where the app violated principles like visibility of system status, recognition over recall, and consistency with user expectations.

— 03

Key Findings

The research confirmed that my frustration was widely shared. Several patterns emerged consistently across all three methods:

  • Users couldn't find included titles — the majority of survey respondents reported difficulty distinguishing between included and paid content in the app.
  • The home page buries subscription value — think-aloud participants consistently scrolled past included content without recognizing it, drawn instead to prominently featured paid titles.
  • No persistent filter existed — users had to re-navigate to find included content every session, relying on recall rather than recognition — a direct violation of a core usability heuristic.
  • Collection management was inconsistent — the "Add to Library" flow differed from familiar patterns on Spotify and Pinterest, causing confusion about how to organize saved titles.
The insight that shaped the design: The problem wasn't that included titles didn't exist in the app — it was that the interface made them invisible by default. The fix didn't require a major redesign, just better filtering and persistent state.

— 04

Prototyping

I started with low-fidelity card-based prototypes — physical cards gave me the flexibility to quickly rearrange, swap, and iterate based on participant reactions without committing to any one direction.

Three distinct concepts were tested:

Prototype 1

An "Included Titles" checkbox filter at the top of the Discover page, appearing after selecting a genre. Keeps filtering contextual to where users are browsing.

Prototype 2

The filter moved to the home page — the first screen users see. Reduces reliance on recall by making included content accessible from the entry point.

Prototype 3

A redesigned "Add to Library" flow with a collections dropdown — consistent with familiar save patterns on Spotify and Pinterest, reducing cognitive load when organizing titles.

— 05

Final Design

The final prototype combined the strongest elements of Prototype 1 and Prototype 3, built in Figma using real Audible interface components for fidelity.

  • Persistent included-titles filter — a checkbox filter on the Discover page that remembers its state as users navigate between genres. If you turn it on in Sci-Fi, it stays on in Romance — eliminating the need to re-apply it every session.
  • Clearer visual hierarchy — included titles are surfaced more prominently within filtered results, reducing the chance users mistake paid content for included content.
  • Collections dropdown on "Add to Library" — tapping the button now reveals a scrollable list of existing collections plus a "New Collection" option, with a confirmation toast at the bottom of the screen matching patterns users already know from other apps.

— 07

Takeaways

This project reinforced a principle I now apply to every design problem: the most impactful UX changes are often not the most dramatic ones. Audible didn't need a visual overhaul — it needed a persistent filter and a more honest information hierarchy. Two targeted changes with significant impact on perceived value.

Running research across three methods also showed me how much each method reveals that the others miss. Survey data told me the problem was widespread. Think-aloud interviews showed me exactly where users got confused. The heuristic evaluation gave me the vocabulary to articulate why. Together they built a much stronger case for the design decisions than any single method would have.
